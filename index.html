Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth) — ss. 394–405

Unfair Dismissal

Eligibility checklist, threshold verification and statement of claim drafting for applications under s.394 of the Fair Work Act 2009.

Eligibility Checklist

Answer each item to confirm eligibility before drafting. Items marked in red may affect the viability of the claim.

Minimum employment period met
6 months (or 12 months if small business employer with fewer than 15 employees)
⚠ Claim may not be available — minimum employment period not satisfied.
Earnings below the High Income Threshold
Current threshold: $183,000 per annum (excl. compulsory super)
⚠ If the employee earns above the HIT and is not covered by a modern award or enterprise agreement, they are not eligible for unfair dismissal. Consider general protections.
Application filed within 21 days of dismissal taking effect
Time limit is strict — FWC may grant extensions only in exceptional circumstances
⚠ Application is out of time. A compelling reason will be required to seek an extension under s.394(3).
Employee is not a casual without regular and systematic engagement
Casual employees must demonstrate regular and systematic engagement with reasonable expectation of continued employment
⚠ Pure casual employees without regular/systematic engagement may not be eligible.
Dismissal was not a genuine redundancy
If it was a genuine redundancy (role no longer required + proper consultation), the claim will be dismissed under s.385(d)
⚠ If this is a genuine redundancy, the UD claim will likely fail. Consider whether proper consultation occurred or redeployment was possible.
Not excluded by an applicable enterprise agreement dispute resolution clause
Some EAs contain dispute resolution procedures that must be exhausted first
⚠ Check the enterprise agreement — there may be a prerequisite dispute resolution process.

Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth) — s.365

General Protections — Involving Dismissal

Eligibility checklist and claim drafting for adverse action applications under s.365 of the Fair Work Act 2009 where dismissal has occurred.

Eligibility Checklist

Confirm all eligibility requirements before filing. General protections claims are broader than unfair dismissal and have no earnings threshold.

The employee was dismissed (as defined in s.12 FWA)
Includes termination by employer, forced resignation or dismissal by demotion. Use GP No Dismissal if no dismissal occurred.
⚠ If no dismissal occurred, this claim must be brought under s.372 (GP Not Involving Dismissal) instead.
Application filed within 21 days of dismissal taking effect
Time limit under s.366 — FWC may extend in exceptional circumstances only
⚠ Application is out of time. Compelling reasons will be needed to seek an extension under s.366(2).
A workplace right, industrial activity or protected attribute was a reason for the dismissal
E.g. making a complaint, exercising a right under an award/EBA, union membership, exercising a workplace right
⚠ Must be able to identify a protected reason that was a reason (not necessarily the only reason) for the dismissal.
The employee is a national system employee
Covered by the Fair Work Act — most private sector employees in Victoria are national system employees
⚠ State public sector employees and some other categories may not be national system employees. Confirm coverage.
Conciliation at FWC is required before Court proceedings
s.365 applications must go through FWC conciliation first. A certificate of unsuccessful conciliation is required to proceed to Court.
⚠ Ensure FWC application is filed first — you cannot go directly to Federal Court without the certificate.
No minimum employment period required
Unlike unfair dismissal, there is no minimum employment period for general protections — even a day-one employee can bring this claim

Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth) — s.372

General Protections — Not Involving Dismissal

Eligibility checklist and claim drafting for adverse action applications under s.372 where no dismissal has occurred.

Eligibility Checklist

For adverse action claims where the employee remains employed. Filed directly in the Federal Court — no FWC conciliation required.

No dismissal has occurred — the employee is still employed
If the employee was dismissed, use GP Involving Dismissal (s.365). This provision covers demotion, reduction in hours, adverse changes to role, etc.
⚠ If dismissal has occurred, this claim must be brought under s.365 instead.
Adverse action has been taken against the employee
E.g. injury to employment, alteration of position to the employee's prejudice, discrimination between employees
⚠ Must identify a specific adverse action — not all unfair workplace treatment constitutes adverse action under the Act.
The adverse action was taken because of a protected reason
Protected reasons include: exercising a workplace right, union membership/activity, making a complaint or inquiry, protected industrial action
⚠ Identify the specific protected reason. The reverse onus provisions (s.361) mean the employer must disprove the connection.
Filed directly in Federal Court (no FWC conciliation required)
Unlike s.365 claims, s.372 applications go directly to the Federal Court — there is no FWC step
⚠ Confirm the correct forum — do not file in FWC for a s.372 matter.
Consider whether injunctive relief is required urgently
If the employer is continuing to take adverse action, an interlocutory injunction may be sought to stop further harm while proceedings continue
⚠ If ongoing adverse action, consider urgency of injunctive relief — take instructions promptly.

Equal Opportunity Act 2010 (Vic) — VCAT

Discrimination Against Employee

Eligibility checklist and claim drafting for discrimination complaints under the Equal Opportunity Act 2010 (Vic) to VCAT.

Eligibility Checklist

Confirm the complaint relates to a protected attribute and prohibited conduct under the EOA 2010.

The conduct relates to a protected attribute under the EOA 2010
Protected attributes include: age, breastfeeding, disability, employment activity, gender identity, industrial activity, lawful sexual activity, marital/relationship status, parental/carer status, physical features, political belief, pregnancy, race, religious belief, sex, sexual orientation, personal association
⚠ Confirm the attribute is specifically protected under the EOA 2010 (Vic) — not all characteristics are protected.
The discrimination occurred in the area of employment (s.16 EOA)
Includes: offers of employment, terms and conditions, opportunities for promotion/transfer/training, dismissal or other detriment
⚠ Must confirm the discriminatory conduct occurred in the specific area of employment under s.16.
Conduct constitutes direct or indirect discrimination
Direct: treated less favourably because of an attribute. Indirect: an unreasonable requirement, condition or practice that disadvantages persons with an attribute.
⚠ Identify whether direct or indirect — the elements and defences differ. Indirect discrimination requires the requirement to be unreasonable.
Complaint lodged with the Victorian Equal Opportunity & Human Rights Commission first
Must first lodge with VEOHRC for conciliation. If conciliation fails, VEOHRC issues a certificate to proceed to VCAT.
⚠ VEOHRC complaint and conciliation certificate is required before VCAT proceedings can commence — ensure this step is completed.
Complaint lodged within 2 years of the alleged discrimination
The limitation period under the EOA 2010 is 2 years from the date of the alleged contravention
⚠ Claim may be out of time — VEOHRC has discretion to accept out-of-time complaints but it is not guaranteed.
Consider whether vicarious liability applies to the employer
Under s.109 EOA, employers are vicariously liable for discriminatory conduct of employees and agents unless all reasonable steps were taken to prevent it
⚠ Assess whether employer took reasonable steps to prevent the conduct — this affects the strength of the vicarious liability argument.

Equal Opportunity Act 2010 (Vic) — s.20

Failure to Make Reasonable Adjustments

Eligibility checklist and claim drafting for failure to make reasonable adjustments for an employee with a disability or attribute under the EOA 2010 (Vic).

Eligibility Checklist

Confirm the employee requested adjustments and the employer failed to make them without unjustifiable hardship.

The employee has a disability or other attribute requiring adjustments
Under s.20 EOA, an employer must make reasonable adjustments for an employee with a disability unless it would impose unjustifiable hardship
⚠ Must establish the attribute (usually disability) that necessitates the adjustment.
The employee requested reasonable adjustments from the employer
The duty to adjust is typically triggered by a request or when the employer is aware of the need — document the request and employer's response
⚠ If no request was made, consider whether the employer had actual or constructive knowledge of the need for adjustment.
The employer failed or refused to make the adjustments
Failure can include outright refusal, unreasonable delay, inadequate adjustments, or dismissal rather than adjustment
⚠ Document the specific adjustments sought and the employer's response in detail.
The adjustments requested were reasonable (not unjustifiable hardship)
Unjustifiable hardship is assessed by considering: cost, nature of the benefit, financial circumstances of the employer and other relevant circumstances
⚠ Employer may raise unjustifiable hardship as a defence — assess the strength of that argument on the facts.
VEOHRC complaint lodged within 2 years of the contravention
Same process as discrimination complaints — VEOHRC conciliation first, then VCAT if unresolved
⚠ Check the date of contravention — the 2 year limitation period applies.

Sex Discrimination Act 1984 (Cth) — ss.28A–28L

Sexual Harassment

Eligibility checklist and claim drafting for sexual harassment complaints under the Sex Discrimination Act 1984 (Cth) and the positive duty to eliminate sexual harassment.

Eligibility Checklist

Confirm the conduct constitutes sexual harassment under the SDA and the appropriate forum for the claim.

The conduct constitutes sexual harassment under s.28A SDA
An unwelcome sexual advance, unwelcome request for sexual favours, or other unwelcome conduct of a sexual nature — where a reasonable person would have anticipated the possibility of offence, humiliation or intimidation
⚠ Both elements must be present: the conduct must be sexual in nature AND unwelcome, assessed against the reasonable person standard.
The harassment occurred in connection with employment (s.28B SDA)
Includes conduct by employer, co-worker, or third party (e.g. customer) where employer is vicariously liable — must be in connection with work
⚠ Confirm the nexus between the conduct and the employment relationship — conduct outside work may not be covered unless connected to employment.
Consider employer vicarious liability and positive duty
Employers are vicariously liable unless all reasonable steps were taken to prevent the harassment. Since 2022, employers also have a positive duty to eliminate sexual harassment (s.47C SDA).
⚠ Assess what steps the employer had in place — policies, training, complaint procedures. Absence of these strengthens vicarious liability.
Complaint lodged with the Australian Human Rights Commission first
Must first lodge with AHRC for conciliation. If terminated, the complainant receives a termination notice and may then proceed to the Federal Court.
⚠ AHRC complaint and termination notice is required before Federal Court proceedings — ensure this step is completed.
AHRC complaint lodged within 2 years of the alleged harassment
The AHRC President may terminate a complaint that is out of time. AHRC has discretion to accept out-of-time complaints in some circumstances.
⚠ Complaint may be out of time — assess whether there are grounds to seek an extension and advise client of risk.
Consider whether FWC stop bullying/harassment order is also appropriate
If the employee is still employed and harassment is ongoing, a FWC application to stop sexual harassment in connection with work (s.527C FWA) may be available in addition to or instead of the SDA complaint
⚠ If still employed, consider urgency of FWC stop order as a faster interim remedy alongside the SDA complaint.
